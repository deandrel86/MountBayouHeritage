We Come From Builders

Mound Bayou is real. In 1887, Isaiah Montgomery and a handful of folks who had been enslaved took a piece of Delta swampland nobody wanted and built something the world still hasn't fully given its flowers to. Their own bank. Their own hospital. Their own schools. Their own everything — because they had to. Because nobody was coming to build it for them.

That's the blood Jinx carries. That's the reason Mama's voice sounds the way it does. "We come from builders, not beggars." — Patricia King

A voice rooted in Southern storytelling

Mount Bayou Heritage is written for the kids who survived systems designed to forget them. It's set in Jackson, Mississippi — a city with real history, real resilience, and real ghosts — and it carries that weight without flinching.

Farish Street in Jackson? Real. At its peak, that street was the whole world for Black Mississippi — doctors, lawyers, barbershops, juke joints, insurance men — all stacked side by side because Jim Crow said they couldn't go anywhere else. So they built their own anywhere else.

Both Mound Bayou and Farish Street got worn down. Not because the people failed — but because the systems around them were designed to make sure they couldn't keep what they built. That's the ground Jinx stands on. That's the reason her rules sound the way they do.

Chapter One: Bite

Mount Bayou Heritage — Opening Scene · Day 1 · 12:13pm

The bell didn't ring on schedule. That's what I noticed first — not the smell of copper that would come later, not the sneakers squealing on tile or the wet clicking of teeth. Just the silence where the bell should've been.

Jim Hill High. 12:13pm. Thirteen minutes to freedom. A hallway full of bodies pretending everything was normal. Adults posted in the wrong places. A line moving too quietly. A room waiting to become a trap.

Jordan walked tight beside me, scanning angles like he was mapping escape routes in real time. His hoodie strings hung loose — he always pulled them when he was nervous, but today they just dangled, untouched. That should've told me something.

"Too many adults near the exits. Coach by the front doors. Security by the office. Nurse in the middle of the hall like she forgot where she was going."

He was right. Coach Anderson stood at the front entrance, but he wasn't watching students. He was watching the parking lot. Hand on his radio. Waiting. The security guard near the office had his badge flipped backward — name facing his chest instead of out. Two teachers stood near the trophy case, whispering. One of them said, "—waiting on instruction—" and the other shushed her fast, eyes darting to see who heard.

"You're spiraling," I said.

"Spiraling saves lives. Denial gets you eaten in act two."

My thumb found my thigh. One tap. Two. The third wouldn't come. My body refused to complete the pattern. That should've told me something too.

Ashley muscled between us, popping her gum. Once. Twice. The rhythm was steady — two pops every five seconds. Metronome-calm. She only popped faster when she was scared.

"Y'all are so dramatic. We get a surprise half-day and your first thought is zombies."

"Name one time they gave us something nice with no catch."

She opened her mouth. Closed it. Popped her gum again — faster. Three pops in four seconds.

"Damn. Girl. Okay."

We passed the cafeteria doors. The smell hit first — industrial green beans, mystery meat, that underlying bleach smell that never quite left the steam tables. Normal. Aggressively normal. But the line was too quiet. Students moving through it without talking, heads down, trays forward. Like assembly line workers clocking in.

I glanced inside. Betty stood on the line, ladle in hand, sweat dripping off her chin. Her eyes were red-rimmed. Pupils too small. Her lips moved like she was praying or cussing or both. Then her voice dropped mid-word. Just dropped an octave. Her shoulders rolled forward. Spine curving in a way that spines don't curve. The tremor in her hands stopped dead. Complete stillness.

Mama's voice brushed the back of my head: Pay attention to quiet people on loud days, Jinx. That's where the truth leaks out.

I should've said something. My hand lifted halfway. Fell. My thumb found my thigh. One tap. Two.

The third never came.

We joined the line instead. Walked straight into the first wave.
